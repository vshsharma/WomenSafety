What is domestic abuse

 

Domestic abuse within a relationship tends to grow over time. In the beginning, violence is rare. At first the abusive partner may make demands or assert dominance, which seem innocent and harmless. But over time, the parties in the relationship change as the abusers' control and violence increase. This often forces the woman to adapt and agree to the increasing demands to avoid further conflicts. Resisting often leads to increased violence. 

 

In general, abuse often begins with psychological violence, such as critical and derogatory comments, which causes the woman to stop believing in herself. Then the physical violence can come as a continuation.

 

It is common for the woman to become isolated and lose her social contacts and stop doing things on her own. The abuser often forbids the woman to interact with her friends and family. This increases the woman’s dependency on her abusive partner.

It is important to know and recognize that abuse comes in different forms and the woman often experience a mix between different types of abuse, such as:

 

Physical abuse - Often called assault. It is when the perpetrator does something to the woman's body that hurts, harms and/or causes discomfort. It can be anything from pushes, kicks and blows to strangulation, restraint or attacking the woman with some kind of object.

 

Psychological abuse – Is when the perpetrator through words and aggressive behavior limits the woman's life. Through psychological abuse the perpetrator can isolate the woman by not letting her interact with other people. Apart from isolation, psychological abuse can include threats, blackmail and control that gradually leads to psychological breakdown. Psychological violence can also mean that threats are made against children, pets or other relatives of the victim. The man can also ridicule or shame his partner, for example the woman's behavior, opinions and / or her way of being.

 

Sexual abuse - Involves forcing someone to engage in, perform or witness sexual acts against their will. It can be about sexual harassment, being forced to watch porn, involuntary touching or rape. It is not uncommon that the victim feeling that she needs to be sexually accessible in order to avoid other forms of violence.

 

Economic abuse - Is when the woman has no control over her own financial resources. It may be that she is not allowed to keep her salary or is prevented from working. The perpetrator can also take out loans in the woman’s name, sometimes against her will, and have access to her bank ID. Ultimately, this reduces the woman’s independence. Aside from that, economic violence can affect a woman's creditworthiness, which can make it challenging for her to obtain a home or take out loans of her own. Often, the perpetrator takes control of the finances and other material assets in order to increase isolation, vulnerability and make it more difficult for the woman to leave the relationship.

 

Emotional abuse - Sometimes this can intertwine with psychological abuse but generally, emotional abuse is a way to control the woman using emotions to criticize, embarrass, shame, blame, or otherwise manipulate the woman with the goal of breaking down her self-esteem and undermining her mental health. Gaslighting is a common form of emotional abuse where the woman starts doubting her own feelings and questioning her reality.

 

Digital abuse - Digital abuse is the use of technologies, such as texting and social networking, to bully, harass, stalk or intimidate a partner. It can also include reading messages or emails and/or figuring out passwords alternatively demanding that the woman provides her passwords as a proof of love / honesty. In some instances, the perpetrator stalks the woman on social media or other apps. Another form of digital abuse is overcommunication, meaning that the perpetrator sends too many text messages and emails to control the woman.

 

Passive physical violence - Is exposing someone to violence that has direct physical consequences. For instance, it can be about denying someone sleep or exposing someone to long-term mental stress that causes physical side effects. In some cases, passive physical violence can be linked to someone else’s vulnerable situation, such as withholding medicine or aids from a person who has a disability or illness.
